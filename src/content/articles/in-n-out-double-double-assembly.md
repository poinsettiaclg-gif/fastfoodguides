[The In-N-Out](/articles/in-n-out-board-station) kitchen operates on a strict hierarchy. The fry cook handles the fryers, the grill cook manages the flat-top, and the Board worker stands between them, responsible for the final assembly of every burger.

While the grill cook gets the glory of managing the meat, the Board is where the real bottleneck happens. The Board worker must memorize every single custom modifier (extra toast, light spread, whole grilled onion) and execute the build with extreme speed.

This is the exact anatomical breakdown of how the classic Double-Double is built at the Board.

## The Foundation: Bun Prep and Spread

Before the meat even leaves the grill, the Board worker receives the freshly toasted buns. The structural integrity of the entire burger relies on what happens in these first five seconds.

1. **The Spatula Swipe:** The Board worker uses a specialized, offset spatula to scoop the proprietary spread. The spread must be applied to the bottom bun using a single, rapid swipe. If the worker double-swipes or uses too much spread, the bottom bun will immediately turn into a soggy mess underneath the weight of the tomatoes.
2. **The Tomato Layer:** The first solid ingredient to hit the bun is always the tomato. This acts as a moisture barrier between the lettuce and the spread. The tomatoes are sliced incredibly thin every morning; a standard Double-Double requires one large slice or two overlapping smaller slices to ensure edge-to-edge coverage.
3. **The Iceberg Crunch:** Hand-leafed iceberg lettuce is placed over the tomatoes. The lettuce provides height and the signature crunch. A critical operational rule at the board is that the lettuce must never hang more than half an inch over the side of the bun, otherwise it interferes with the wrapping process.

## The Hand-Off and The Wrap

Once the "bottoms" are fully dressed, they wait on the stainless steel cutting board for the grill cook to transfer the meat.

The grill cook uses a massive spatula to slide the two patties (with the melted American cheese fused between them) directly onto the waiting bed of lettuce. Immediately, the Board worker caps the burger with the toasted top bun.

The final step is the wrapper. In-N-Out burgers are only wrapped halfway. The Board worker uses a diagonal folding technique with the wax paper to create a structural "diaper" that catches the grease and spread while leaving the top half of the burger exposed for immediate visual appeal. 

If the Board worker is too slow, the grill cook has nowhere to put the finished meat. The entire store's drive-thru times are dictated by the rhythm between these two stations.
