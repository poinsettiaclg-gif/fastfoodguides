In-N-Out Burger is famous for its simple, high-quality menu. But anyone who has lived in California knows that the real menu isn't printed on the wall. The "Secret Menu" is the worst-kept secret in the fast-food world, and the undisputed king of that menu is **Animal Style Fries**.

As a former QSR manager, I'm always fascinated by how restaurants integrate off-menu items into their highly regimented assembly lines. At In-N-Out, Animal Style Fries are such a high-volume request that the kitchen has specific procedures and tools just for building them.

Let me lay out the process:

## Step 1: The Fry Station Drop

The foundation of the dish is, of course, the fries. In-N-Out is famous for using fresh Kennebec potatoes, cut in-store. 

When an order for Animal Style Fries comes through the POS system (it prints on the ticket simply as "ANML FRY"), the fry cook knows they need a standard portion. The fries are cooked in 100% sunflower oil. 

<div class="callout callout-tip">
<strong>Note:</strong> Because Animal Style Fries are heavy and wet, the fry cook will often try to pull a slightly crispier batch for this order, leaving them in the oil for an extra 15-30 seconds if the rush allows it. This prevents the dish from turning into mashed potatoes under the weight of the toppings.
</div>


## Step 2: The Red Boat

Standard fries are scooped into a paper sleeve. Animal Style Fries are built in a wide, rectangular red cardboard boat. The fry cook dumps the crispy fries into the boat, spreading them out to maximize the surface area. 

This boat is then passed down the line to the "Board" station—the area where burgers are dressed and wrapped.

## Step 3: The Cheese Melt

The defining characteristic of Animal Style Fries is the melted American cheese. In-N-Out uses a high-quality, high-melt American cheese slice. 

The Board associate takes two slices of cheese and places them directly on top of the hot fries. 

But how does it melt so perfectly without a microwave or an oven? 

<div class="callout callout-tip">
<strong>Insider Secret:</strong> The associate slides the red boat directly onto the edge of the flat-top grill, or places an aluminum dome over the boat if they are in a hurry. The ambient heat from the massive 400°F burger grill rapidly melts the cheese over the fries in about 15 seconds.
</div>


## Step 4: The Grilled Onions

While the cheese is melting, the grill cook is managing a massive pile of finely diced, caramelized onions at the back of the flat-top. 

The associate takes a specialized metal scoop, grabs a precise portion of these dark, sweet grilled onions, and drops them directly onto the newly melted cheese. The heat from the onions further fuses the cheese to the fries.

## Step 5: The Spread

The final step is the signature In-N-Out "Spread." While many people assume it is just Thousand Island dressing, it is actually a proprietary recipe containing mayonnaise, ketchup, sweet pickle relish, and a splash of white vinegar to cut through the fat of the cheese and burgers.

The associate takes a squeeze bottle of Spread and applies a heavy, zig-zag pattern over the top of the onions and cheese. 

The dish is then immediately handed off to the expeditor, placed on a red serving tray or in a bag, and handed to the customer with a fork. It is a messy, calorie-dense masterpiece that showcases how In-N-Out's flexible kitchen line can handle complex customization without slowing down the drive-thru.
