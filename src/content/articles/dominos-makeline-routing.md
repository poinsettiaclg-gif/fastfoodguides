Domino’s Pizza is widely considered to be a tech company that happens to sell pizza. Since introducing their proprietary PULSE operating system in the early 2000s, they have digitized and tracked every single second of a pizza's life—from the moment you click "Order" on the app to the moment the driver rings your doorbell.

As a former manager, I can tell you that the stress of a Friday night dinner rush at Domino's isn't just about making 300 pizzas; it’s about beating the digital clocks scattered across the kitchen.

Here is how the Domino's digital makeline and routing system actually works, and the secrets behind the famous "Pizza Tracker."

## The Digital Makeline (The Heart of the Kitchen)

When an order enters the system, it immediately pops up on a large flat-screen monitor suspended above the pizza prep table (the **Makeline**). 

The Makeline screen displays the orders in a color-coded grid. But it’s not just a digital ticket—it is a timer. 
Domino's tracks a metric called **Load Time** (or Make Time). This is the exact amount of time it takes for an employee to read the order on the screen, [stretch the dough](/articles/chain/dominos/dominos-dough-stretching), apply the sauce and cheese, portion the toppings, and literally load the pizza into the conveyor oven.

The corporate goal for Load Time is usually **3 minutes**. 
- If an order sits on the Makeline screen for 1 minute, it is Green.
- At 2 minutes, it flashes Yellow.
- If it crosses 3 minutes, it turns Red, and the store's average Load Time takes a massive hit.

To clear an order from the screen, the pizza maker must physically press a button on a bump bar (a small keyboard) mounted above the ingredients. 

<div class="callout callout-warning">
<strong>The "Bumping" Trick:</strong> Because managers' bonuses are often tied to Load Times, some unscrupulous managers encourage "ghost bumping." This is when an employee clears the order off the digital screen <em>before</em> the pizza is actually made, just to keep the timer green. If your Domino's Tracker says your pizza is "In the Oven" but it actually takes 45 minutes to arrive, ghost bumping is usually the culprit.
</div>

## The Cut Table (The Oven Tender)

The conveyor ovens at Domino's operate at around 450°F to 475°F, and a pizza takes exactly 6.5 to 7.5 minutes to travel from one end to the other. 

At the end of the oven stands the **Oven Tender** (or Cut Table operator). Above them is another digital screen. 
When a pizza falls out of the oven, the Oven Tender boxes it, cuts it, and looks at their screen to match the pizza to the correct order ticket. 

Once the order is complete, they print the physical receipt label, slap it on the box, and move it to the heat rack. This action updates the Domino's Tracker to say: *"Your order is being prepped for delivery."*

## The Dispatch System (Routing the Drivers)

The final digital hurdle is the **Dispatch Station**. This is a screen located near the back door where delivery drivers congregate.

Domino's tracks **Out the Door (OTD)** time. The goal is for a pizza to leave the store less than 15 minutes after the customer ordered it. 

When a driver returns to the store, they look at the Dispatch screen. The PULSE system is incredibly smart—it uses proprietary routing algorithms to automatically group deliveries that are geographically close to each other. 
For example, if two orders are going to the same apartment complex, the system highlights them together and suggests a "Double" (taking two orders at once). 

The driver clicks their name on the touch screen, selects the orders they are taking, and hits "Route." 
This action triggers the final stage of the Domino's Tracker: *"Your driver has left the store."*

## Beating the Clock

Operating a Domino's during the Super Bowl or Halloween requires absolute adherence to the screens. The Makeline crew has to stretch dough in under 20 seconds. The [Oven Tender](/articles/chain/dominos/dominos-oven-tender-role) has to catch, box, and cut pizzas at a rate of two per minute without dropping any on the floor. And the drivers have to route and hustle to their cars.

The entire ecosystem is designed to minimize the time a pizza spends sitting still. The technology enforces the speed, and the employees simply have to keep up.
