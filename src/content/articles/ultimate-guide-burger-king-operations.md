Burger King's entire brand identity is built around the phrase "Flame-Grilled." To execute this at a massive, global scale requires a kitchen architecture that is fundamentally different from competitors like [McDonald's](/articles/chain/mcdonalds) or [Wendy's](/articles/chain/wendys), who use flat-top griddles. 

At Burger King, the centerpiece of the kitchen is a roaring, automated firebox. Everything in the back-of-house workflow orbits around this massive piece of machinery and the "Have It Your Way" assembly line.

This guide serves as your central hub for understanding the gritty reality of running a Burger King kitchen during peak hours.

## The Flame Broiler

You cannot understand Burger King without understanding the Broiler. This is an enormous, gas-powered oven with a continuous chain-link conveyor belt running through it. 

Instead of a cook manually flipping burgers on a flat-top, the cook's primary job is simply to feed the machine. They drop frozen patties onto the belt, and the machine drags the meat through open gas flames, ensuring a perfectly consistent cook time and that signature charred flavor.

<strong>Operational Reality:</strong> While the broiler is highly efficient during a rush, it is a nightmare if it breaks down. It is also one of the most dangerous pieces of equipment in the fast-food industry due to the open flames and grease buildup.
</div>

## The Assembly Line and The Expeditor

Burger King's other famous slogan is "Have It Your Way." This promise of deep customization means their assembly line (the "board") must be highly adaptable. 

When a customized Whopper order comes in, the board workers assemble it using heated holding cabinets (the PHU) that store the freshly broiled meat. But the most critical position in this entire workflow isn't the person making the burger; it's the person bagging it. 

This role is known as the **Expeditor** (or Expo). The Expeditor stands at the end of the production line. They are the final quality check, the bagger, and the primary communicator. If a customized burger is made incorrectly, the Expeditor must catch it before it hits the drive-thru window. If the drive-thru needs more fries, the Expeditor shouts the call to the fry station. 

A strong Expeditor can carry a weak kitchen through a lunch rush. A weak Expeditor will cause a strong kitchen to collapse into chaos.
