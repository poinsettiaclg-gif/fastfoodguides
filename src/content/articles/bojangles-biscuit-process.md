## The Hardest Job in the Kitchen

If you work at Bojangles, there is one position that commands more respect than any other: The Biscuit Maker. 

While many fast-food chains have transitioned to frozen, pre-formed biscuits that just need to be baked, Bojangles has stubbornly stuck to the traditional, from-scratch method. The process is so rigorous that they hold annual competitions to crown the "Master Biscuit Maker."

![Bojangles Biscuit Station](../../assets/images/bojangles/biscuit-station.png)

## The 49-Step Process

Bojangles claims there are exactly 49 steps to making their perfect biscuit. While that sounds like marketing hyperbole, the actual process *is* incredibly precise.

### 1. The Flour and the Fat
It starts with a proprietary self-rising flour blend. The most critical step is cutting the shortening into the flour. This is done by hand (or with a specialized mixer depending on the store volume) until the mixture resembles coarse crumbs. This fat distribution is what creates the flaky layers.

### 2. The Buttermilk Fold
Cold buttermilk is added. The biscuit maker cannot overwork the dough; if they do, the gluten develops too much, resulting in a tough, hockey-puck biscuit. It must be folded gently, just enough to bring the dough together.

### 3. The Knead and Roll
The dough is turned out onto a heavily floured surface. It's kneaded exactly a specific number of times (usually around 15 folds) to build the layers. Then, it's rolled out to a precise thickness. Bojangles uses special rolling pins with guides on the ends to ensure the dough is exactly the right height.

### 4. The Cut and Bake
Using a specific biscuit cutter, the maker punches straight down and pulls straight up. *Never twist.* Twisting seals the edges and prevents the biscuit from rising high and fluffy. They are placed touching each other on the baking sheet so they rise *up* instead of spreading *out*.

## The 20-Minute Rule

Bojangles has a strict quality control rule: Biscuits are only good for 20 minutes after coming out of the oven. 

During the morning rush, the Biscuit Maker is in a state of constant motion. They are mixing a new batch while the previous batch is in the oven, and the batch before that is being served. It requires a level of stamina, timing, and flour-covered precision that makes it one of the most demanding prep jobs in the QSR industry.
