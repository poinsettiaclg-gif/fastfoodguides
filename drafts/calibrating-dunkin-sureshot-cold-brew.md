If your beverage station is backing up the Makeline because the expeditor is kicking iced coffees back for color issues, your SureShot is out of spec. Guessing dairy throws is a rookie mistake that tanks your speed of service and spikes your pour cost. Today, we are dialing in the exact gram-weight volumes for the SureShot dispenser and running the mandatory line-flushing protocols for the cold brew tap system. 

Stop eyeballing the cup lines. Get a digital scale and a wrench.

## Dialing in the SureShot: Dairy Throw Gram-Weight Specs

The SureShot liquid dispenser relies on peristaltic pumps that degrade over time, meaning the tubing gets crushed and fluid velocity drops. If you aren't doing weekly gram-weight calibration, your 'Medium Cream' is short-pouring by at least 15%. 

### The Calibration Setup

You need a calibrated digital scale capable of reading down to 0.1 grams. Do not use the bulky prep scale from the back of house. 

1. Power on the digital scale and place a standard empty Small Iced cup on the platform.
2. Hit the Tare button. It must read exactly `0.0g`.
3. Unlock the SureShot programming mode by holding the hidden reset toggle under the digital display until the screen flashes `PROG`.

### Executing the Volume Test

We calibrate using the 'Cream' setting because milk and skim have different viscosities. Always calibrate with a fresh, properly chilled dairy bag (FIFO rules apply—don't use a bag that's been sitting at ambient temp during a truck pull).

1. Place the tared cup under the nozzle.
2. Press the **Small Cream** button. Let the throw finish completely.
3. Weigh the cup. The target spec for a Small Cream throw is **18.0g (± 0.5g)**.
4. If the weight is off, use the `+` and `-` arrows on the SureShot interface to adjust the pump actuation time in milliseconds. 
5. Dump the cup, re-tare, and test the **Medium (27.0g)** and **Large (36.0g)** throws. 

Once your gram-weights are dialed in, lock the programming interface. If you skip this, your KDS metrics will suffer because you'll be dumping and remaking drinks all through peak.

## Purging the Tap: Cold Brew and Nitro Draft Protocols

The tap system is a closed-loop pressurized draft lines setup. When organic buildup occurs in the lines, it causes turbulent flow, which destroys the nitrogen cascade in your Nitro Cold Brew and causes the standard Cold Brew to pour with a sour, oxidized bite.

### Mandatory Line-Flushing Procedures

You do this every time you swap a keg, or at bare minimum, once every 72 hours.

1. Shut off the primary nitrogen valve at the regulator.
2. Disconnect the liquid line from the Cold Brew keg (or BIB coupler, depending on your franchise's specific distribution model).
3. Connect the flushing canister filled with approved sanitizing solution (mixed to exactly 200 PPM).
4. Open the tap handle at the beverage station and let the line bleed until the liquid runs clear. 
5. Close the tap and let the sanitizer dwell in the lines for exactly 10 minutes. 
6. Flush with cold, filtered water for 60 seconds to clear all chemical residue.

### Nitrogen Pressure Regulation

A perfect Nitro Cold Brew cascade relies entirely on precise gas regulation. If your pressure is wrong, you're pouring flat mud or a cup of shaving cream.

1. Check the primary regulator on the nitrogen tank. It must be locked precisely between **28 and 32 PSI**.
2. If the gauge reads below 28 PSI, check for micro-leaks at the O-rings on the gas disconnects. 
3. After reconnecting the keg, bleed the pressure relief valve (PRV) for two seconds to purge any ambient oxygen from the headspace. 
4. Pull a test pint. The cascade should settle in exactly 45 to 60 seconds, leaving a tight, micro-foam head of about 0.5 inches.

Keep the lines clean, keep the pressure tight, and maintain your equipment. The POS doesn't care if your machinery is failing, it just keeps spitting out tickets. Do the maintenance or drown in the rush.
